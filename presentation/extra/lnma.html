Living systematic review (LSR): Why, what, how and when

Keeping evidence up to date

The goal of evidence synthesis, at least from a health research perspective, is to provide the best evidence that are available. Methods for evidence synthesis are well developed (Moher et al. 2009; Higgins et al. 2012), usually being resource intensive, which makes difficult to keep it up to date as new evidence becomes available (Bastian, Glasziou, and Chalmers 2010).

Traditionally, SRs are not updated or updated sporadically (Garner et al. 2016). This leaves gaps which may be missing new research that are important.

The “Living Systematic Review Network” has published an overview of LSRs and living guidance recommendations (Elliott et al. 2017; Thomas et al. 2017; Simmonds et al. 2017; Akl et al. 2017)

What is a LSR?

  • A SR which is continuously updated
  • An approach to do it and not a formal methodology
  • Explicit predetermined frequency of search and review updating

When is appropiate?

  1. Priority in the context of decision-making
  2. Certainty in the existing evidence is low or very low
  3. There is likely to be new research evidence

How to perform a LSR?

  1. Protocol
  2. Produce a “baseline” SR, aiming for a smooth transition to a LSR
    • Search
    • Project management
    • Data science
    • Evidence synthesis
    • Quality of evidence (GRADE)
  3. Publication
  4. Link to other “living” activities

Covid-19: LSR and NMA

Background I

  • The number of randomized clinical trials evaluating vaccines, prophylaxis, and treatments reached 1,000 before August 2020, and is currently at 3,250.
  • SRs labeled as “rapid” proliferated in the early days of the pandemic, and 1,700 of the COVID-19 relevant reviews are described as rapid.
  • Unfortunately, many reviewers use the term “rapid” to justify conducting sub-optimal SRs, including the use of single reviewers for critical tasks such as data abstraction, risk of bias assessment, and assessment of the certainty of evidence.

Background II

  • Our COVID-19 Living NMA (LNMA) addressing drug prophylaxis, antiviral antibodies and cellular products, and drug treatments (Siemieniuk et al. 2020, 2021; Bartoszko et al. 2021) is a constantly updated SR that has informed the development of a number of World Health Organization (WHO) practice recommendations (Agarwal et al. 2020).
  • With, as of February, 2022, 592 unique trials reported in 992 publications, our group have collected a large amount of data that allows us to address important methodological questions.
  • The need to maintain an up to date, dynamic system for evidence synthesis in the context of COVID-19 can be facilitated using new technologies which comprise both human and machine effort.

Data science in the context of the COVID-19 LNMA

  • SR activities can be broken down into “micro-tasks”. Machine automation can assist with some of these tasks, including routine searching, eligibility assessment, identification and retrieval of full-text reports, and others (Thomas et al. 2017).
  • Updated every 2 months.
  • By dividing the work into micro-tasks, our LNMA may be conducted more efficiently among a wider range of people.
  • Our process automation is written in R and Python, and can be found in our github repository.

Conclusions

We have provided an overview of the workflow used in our COVID-19 LNMA. Automation scripts were created with the objective of achieving a continual update of the most relevant evidence. We also aimed to remain flexible to incorporate new developments in the broader evidence ecosystem.
